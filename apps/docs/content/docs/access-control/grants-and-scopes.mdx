# Grants & scopes

A **grant** is the unit of access. Every grant answers four questions:

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | a principal — a **user**, or a **team** <Enterprise /> (everyone on the team) |
| **What** | a **role** (a bundle of permissions) **or** a **single permission** (e.g. `spec:deploy`) |
| **Where** | a **scope** — the whole organization, or one specific resource (a zone, spec, runner, …) |
| **Effect** | **allow** (the default) or an explicit **deny** |

This is the same shape as an AWS IAM statement, and you create them through the
[Access portal](/access-portal) <Enterprise /> (or, in Community, the single owner already holds everything).

## Scope and inheritance

Resources form a hierarchy: **Org → Zone → Spec** (runners, cloud identities, and connectors hang directly off
the org). A grant flows *down* that hierarchy:

- A grant on the **organization** applies to every resource in it.
- A grant on a **zone** applies to that zone *and the specs inside it*.
- A grant on a single **spec** applies to just that spec.

So "let Dana view this one zone" automatically lets Dana view the specs in it — you grant once, at the right
level, instead of enumerating children.

<Callout type="info">
  This inheritance is the same in both editions and in both engines. Community resolves it by walking the
  `resource_hierarchy` table; the OpenFGA engine resolves it through relationship tuples. The decision is
  identical.
</Callout>

## Explicit deny — the exception you actually need

Inheritance is convenient until you need an *exception*: "Dana can view this zone's specs — **except this one
spec**." That's an **explicit deny**.

Deny has AWS-IAM semantics:

- A **deny always wins** over an allow, no matter how the allow was granted (directly or inherited). There's no
  "most specific wins" tie-breaking — if any deny covers the resource, access is refused.
- A deny **inherits down** the hierarchy just like an allow. A deny on a *zone* excludes all of its specs; a
  deny on a single *spec* excludes just that one.

### Worked example

Give Dana read access to a zone, then carve out one spec:

<Steps>
<Step>**Allow** the `viewer` role on **zone `Z`**. Dana can now view `Z` and all of its specs.</Step>
<Step>**Deny** the `spec:view` permission on **spec `S`** (one of the specs in `Z`).</Step>
<Step>Result: Dana sees zone `Z` and its *other* specs, but **not `S`**. The deny on `S` overrides the inherited allow.</Step>
</Steps>

The decision rule, in one line:

> **allowed ⟺ an allow covers the resource (directly or via an ancestor) AND no deny covers it.**

## Role grants vs single-permission grants

Two ways to say "what":

- **Grant a role** — the managed-policy model. Roles bundle permissions; assign the whole bundle. Use built-in
  roles, or author a [custom role](/access-control/custom-roles) <Enterprise /> for an exact set.
- **Grant a single permission** — the inline model. Attach one `resource:action` (e.g. `spec:deploy`) without a
  role. Handy for one-off exceptions, and for denies (deny exactly `spec:view` on `S`, nothing more).

Editing a role's permissions instantly re-derives every grant that uses it — there's no need to re-assign.
