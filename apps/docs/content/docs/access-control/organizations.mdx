# Organizations & Teams

An **organization** is the tenancy boundary: every project, runner, cloud identity, and job belongs to exactly one organization, and members of that organization see only its resources. Your **active organization** is half of your [actor](/access-control) identity — it's the scope every permission check runs against.

## Community: your personal organization

<Callout type="info">
In the free Community edition there is **one organization per user** — your *personal organization* — and you are its sole `owner`. You never see another member. Everything you create is scoped to it automatically.
</Callout>

Under the hood, your personal organization's id is simply your user id, and you're given a single org-wide `owner` grant when your account is created. That's the entire setup — no organization to create, no members to manage. The full roles-and-grants engine is present and enforcing; it just has one member: you.

## Enterprise: multi-member organizations

The commercial Enterprise edition turns the organization into a real, shared, multi-member tenancy boundary:

<Cards>
  <Card title="Organizations">
    Named, shared tenancy boundaries. The creator becomes the <code>owner</code>. All existing resources stay scoped to their organization.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Members">
    Users who belong to an organization, each holding a role (<code>owner</code> / <code>admin</code> / <code>operator</code> / <code>viewer</code>) that determines what they can do.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Invitations">
    Invite a teammate by email; they accept and join with the role you choose.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Teams">
    Group members so grants can target a whole team at once (rather than each user).
  </Card>
</Cards>

### Lifecycle

<Steps>
<Step>
**Create an organization.** The creator is automatically its `owner` — they get an org-wide owner grant, so the authorization engine recognizes them as fully privileged within the new org immediately.
</Step>
<Step>
**Invite members by email.** Each invitation carries the role the new member will hold. The invitee accepts and becomes a member.
</Step>
<Step>
**Members act within the org.** Every request a member makes is checked against *their* role in *that* organization — an `operator` can deploy infrastructure but not manage billing; a `viewer` can only look.
</Step>
<Step>
**Scope flows everywhere.** Because the member's organization is their active scope, list views, actions, and the database isolation layer all key off it — a member of org A can never see org B's resources.
</Step>
</Steps>

### How a member's role becomes access

When a member makes a request, the system resolves their **active organization** and checks their grant in it:

```text
member: carol, role = operator, in org "Acme"
  request: deploy project "Acme/payments"
  ⇒ operator includes project:deploy  → ALLOWED (and scoped to Acme only)

  request: invite a new member
  ⇒ operator does NOT include member:manage_members → FORBIDDEN
```

Roles compose with the [Org → Project hierarchy](/access-control/roles-and-permissions#inheritance-the-org--project-hierarchy): you can give someone `operator` org-wide, or narrow them to just one project.

## Tenancy isolation

Organization isolation isn't only an application-level check. The database enforces it too: every query runs with the active organization in scope, and PostgreSQL Row-Level Security only returns rows for that organization. So even a flaw in the application layer cannot surface another organization's data. See [Security → Row-Level Security](/concepts/security).

<Callout type="info">
A member can belong to **several organizations** and switch between them with the **organization switcher** in
the dashboard header; the active organization scopes everything the PDP and Row-Level Security enforce.
[**SSO**](/access-control/sso) sign-in, [**teams**](/access-control/teams), the
[**invitation flow**](/access-control/members-and-invitations), and the [**access portal**](/access-portal)
are all part of the Enterprise edition.
</Callout>

## Relationship to roles

Organizations are *where* you have access; [roles](/access-control/roles-and-permissions) are *what* that access is. A member always has both: an organization (the scope) and a role within it (the capability). Changing someone from `viewer` to `operator` changes what they can do; moving them between organizations changes what they can see.
